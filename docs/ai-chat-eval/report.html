The Rhozly reply template — what "consistency" is judged against

Baked into the assistant's system prompt (agent-chat/rules.ts); canonical copy in reply-template.md.

Every Garden AI answer follows the same five-part shape, so a gardener always knows

where to look. Parts 2–5 appear only when they apply — a simple factual question

gets part 1 (and maybe 5) alone, in one or two sentences.

1. Bottom line — one plain sentence that directly answers the question or states

what has been prepared. Always first. Never a heading, never a preamble.

2. Detail bullets — only when the answer genuinely has parts (max ~6):

  • When: late summer, right after flowering
  • How: cut back a third, never into old wood
  • Watch for: frost on new growth

Short bold labels (When / How / How much / Why / Watch for) so the eye can jump

straight to the fact it needs.

3. Garden line — only when the assistant read the user's data this turn:

🔎 Checked: your Shed (2 tomatoes) · this week's tasks · weather

4. Action line — only when an action was staged for confirmation:

🔧 Ready to confirm: water Raised Bed A & B every 3 days

When a follow-up step depends on this one (e.g. linking an ailment that's being

added), the line also says what will be staged right after the user confirms.

5. Next step — at most one offer or question, on its own line:

→ Want me to set up a fortnightly feeding reminder too?

Why this shape

  • Bottom-line-first means nobody reads a paragraph to find a yes/no.
  • Labelled bullets make facts scannable and consistent across every answer.
  • 🔎 / 🔧 / → are fixed markers: what was *read*, what's *ready to apply*, what

*could happen next* — the same place, every reply.

  • One offer max keeps replies calm — no stacked questions.

N04Diagnosisexpected: knowledge — no tool expected
Q My tomato leaves are turning yellow — what's wrong?
Yellowing tomato leaves are most often caused by watering issues, a nutrient deficiency, or pests.

Here’s how to figure out which it is:
- **Watering:** Check the soil about two inches deep. If it's bone dry, you're likely underwatering. If it's soggy, you could be overwatering, which suffocates the roots. Tomatoes in beds and containers need consistent moisture, not a flood-and-drought cycle.
- **Nutrient Deficiency:** This is very common. The location of the yellowing is the main clue:
- If the **oldest, lowest leaves** are turning yellow first, it's likely a **Nitrogen** deficiency.
- If the **newest, youngest leaves** at the top are yellowing (especially between the veins), it points to a lack of **Iron** or **Magnesium**.
- A balanced liquid tomato feed every couple of weeks, like your "Feed Tomatoes & Fruit" schedule, should prevent this.
- **Pests & Diseases:** Look closely at the undersides of the leaves. Tiny insects like aphids or spider mites can suck the sap and cause yellow stippling. Fungal diseases often present as yellow spots that may turn brown.

Q What's the cheapest way to improve my heavy clay soil?
The cheapest and best way to improve heavy clay soil is by adding lots of organic matter.

This improves the soil structure by binding the fine clay particles into larger clumps, which creates air pockets and improves drainage.

- **Homemade Compost:** This is the best option because it's free. Use a mix of kitchen scraps (greens) and garden waste (browns) to create a nutrient-rich soil conditioner.
- **Leaf Mould:** Simply rake up fallen leaves in autumn into a pile or a bag with holes in it. After a year or two, it rots down into a fantastic, free soil improver.
- **Green Manures:** Sowing a fast-growing cover crop like clover, phacelia, or winter tares in autumn is very cheap. You let them grow and then chop them down, allowing the foliage to rot on the surface or digging it into the top layer of soil.
- **Well-rotted Manure:** If you live near any farms or stables, you can often get manure for free if you're able to collect it yourself. Make sure it's well-rotted (at least 6-12 months old) as fresh manure can scorch plants.
- **How to Apply:** The easiest method is to spread a thick layer (2-4 inches) of your chosen organic matter on the surface in autumn and let the worms and weather work it into the soil for you over winter.
- **What to Avoid:** Don't add sand. It's a common myth, but adding sand to clay can create a hard, concrete-like substance that makes the problem even worse.

That's a common and frustrating problem, almost always caused by a lack of pollination.

Courgette plants have separate male and female flowers, and pollen needs to get from a male to a female for a fruit to develop. If a female flower isn't pollinated, the plant aborts the tiny fruit and the flower falls off.

- **Identify the Flowers:** You can tell the difference easily. Female flowers have a tiny, immature courgette swelling at their base, right behind the petals. Male flowers just have a straight, thin stem.
