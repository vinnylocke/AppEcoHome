The Rhozly reply template — what "consistency" is judged against

Baked into the assistant's system prompt (agent-chat/rules.ts); canonical copy in reply-template.md.

Every Garden AI answer follows the same five-part shape, so a gardener always knows

where to look. Parts 2–5 appear only when they apply — a simple factual question

gets part 1 (and maybe 5) alone, in one or two sentences.

1. Bottom line — one plain sentence that directly answers the question or states

what has been prepared. Always first. Never a heading, never a preamble.

2. Detail bullets — only when the answer genuinely has parts (max ~6):

  • When: late summer, right after flowering
  • How: cut back a third, never into old wood
  • Watch for: frost on new growth

Short bold labels (When / How / How much / Why / Watch for) so the eye can jump

straight to the fact it needs.

3. Garden line — only when the assistant read the user's data this turn:

🔎 Checked: your Shed (2 tomatoes) · this week's tasks · weather

4. Action line — only when an action was staged for confirmation:

🔧 Ready to confirm: water Raised Bed A & B every 3 days

When a follow-up step depends on this one (e.g. linking an ailment that's being

added), the line also says what will be staged right after the user confirms.

5. Next step — at most one offer or question, on its own line:

→ Want me to set up a fortnightly feeding reminder too?

Why this shape

  • Bottom-line-first means nobody reads a paragraph to find a yes/no.
  • Labelled bullets make facts scannable and consistent across every answer.
  • 🔎 / 🔧 / → are fixed markers: what was *read*, what's *ready to apply*, what

*could happen next* — the same place, every reply.

  • One offer max keeps replies calm — no stacked questions.

Q I'm brand new to gardening and feeling a bit overwhelmed. Where do I even start?
It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed at first, but gardening is incredibly rewarding! Let's get you started.

- **Start Small:** Don't try to do too much at once. Pick one or two easy-to-grow plants and a small area, like a single raised bed or a few containers.
- **Choose Easy Plants:** For beginners, I recommend plants that are generally forgiving and don't require constant attention. Some great options include:
- **Herbs:** Basil, mint (be careful, it can spread!), parsley, chives.
- **Leafy Greens:** Lettuce, spinach, kale.
- **Root Vegetables:** Radishes, carrots (in deeper soil).
- **Flowers:** Marigolds, zinnias, sunflowers.
- **Location, Location, Location:** Most vegetables and many flowers need at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day. Observe your garden space throughout the day to see where the sun hits.
- **Good Soil is Key:** Healthy plants start with healthy soil. If you're planting in the ground, you may want to amend the soil with compost. For containers, use a good quality potting mix.
- **Water Wisely:** Overwatering is a common beginner mistake. Check the soil moisture by sticking your finger about an inch deep. If it feels dry, it's time to water. Water the base of the plant, not the leaves.
- **Learn as You Grow:** Gardening is a continuous learning process. Don't be afraid to make mistakes – they're part of the journey!

N04Diagnosisexpected: knowledge — no tool expected
Q My tomato leaves are turning yellow — what's wrong?
Yellowing tomato leaves can be caused by several factors, but given it's summer, it's most likely related to nutrient deficiency or overwatering.

Here's a breakdown of common causes and solutions:

* **Nutrient Deficiency (Nitrogen):** This is a very common cause of yellowing leaves, especially on older, lower leaves. Nitrogen is mobile in the plant, so it moves to new growth, leaving older leaves deficient.
* **How:** Feed your tomatoes with a balanced liquid fertilizer or one specifically formulated for tomatoes. Ensure it contains nitrogen. Apply according to the product's instructions, typically every 2-3 weeks during the growing season.
* **Overwatering:** Consistently wet soil can lead to root rot and prevent the plant from taking up nutrients, causing yellowing.
* **How:** Check the soil moisture before watering. Stick your finger about an inch or two into the soil; if it feels moist, wait to water. Ensure good drainage in the area where your tomatoes are planted.
* **Underwatering:** While less common in summer if you're watering regularly, prolonged dry spells can also stress the plant and cause yellowing.
* **How:** Water deeply and less frequently, ensuring the soil has a chance to dry out slightly between waterings. Mulching around the base of the plants can help retain soil moisture.
* **Disease (e.g., Fusarium Wilt, Verticillium Wilt):** These soil-borne fungal diseases can cause yellowing, wilting, and eventual death of the plant. Often, the yellowing starts on one side of a leaf or one side of the plant.
* **How:** Unfortunately, there's no cure for these diseases once a plant is infected. Remove and destroy infected plants to prevent spread. In the future, choose disease-resistant varieties and practice crop rotation.
* **Pests (e.g., Spider Mites, Aphids):** Heavy infestations can suck the sap from the leaves, causing them to yellow and become stippled.
* **How:** Inspect the undersides of leaves for tiny pests or fine webbing. Treat with insecticidal soap or neem oil if an infestation is found.

To take softwood cuttings from your hydrangea, follow these steps:

3. **How to take cuttings:**

4. **Prepare the cuttings:**
* Remove the leaves from the lower half of the cutting.
* If the cutting has large leaves, you can cut them in half horizontally to reduce water loss.
* You can optionally dip the cut end in rooting hormone to encourage faster root development.

5. **Potting the cuttings:**
* Fill small pots or a seed tray with a free-draining potting mix, such as a 50:50 mix of perlite and peat moss or a commercial seed-starting mix.
* Make a small hole in the center of the mix with a pencil or dibber.
* Insert the cutting into the hole, ensuring the leaf nodes that were below the soil line are covered.
* Gently firm the compost around the base of the cutting.

6. **Provide the right conditions:**
* Water the cuttings well after potting.
* Place the pots in a propagator or cover them with a clear plastic bag to maintain high humidity. Ensure the leaves don't touch the plastic.
