Q How do I take softwood cuttings from my hydrangea?
To take softwood cuttings from your hydrangea, follow these steps:

1. **Timing:** The best time to take softwood cuttings is in late spring or early summer, typically May or June, when the new growth is flexible but starting to firm up.

2. **Selection:** Choose healthy, vigorous stems that are actively growing. Look for stems that have recently finished flowering or are about to.

3. **Cutting:** Using sharp, clean secateurs or a knife, take cuttings that are about 10-15 cm (4-6 inches) long. Make the cut just below a leaf node (where a leaf attaches to the stem).

4. **Preparation:**
* Remove the lower leaves from the cutting, leaving only the top 2-3 leaves. If the remaining leaves are very large, you can cut them in half horizontally to reduce water loss.
* If there's a flower bud, pinch it out to direct the plant's energy into root development.
* You can optionally dip the cut end in rooting hormone to encourage faster and more reliable root formation.

5. **Potting:**
* Fill small pots or seed trays with a well-draining potting mix. A good mix is equal parts peat-free compost and perlite or coarse sand.
* Make a hole in the soil with a pencil or dibber.
* Insert the cutting into the hole, ensuring the lowest leaf node is below the soil surface. Gently firm the soil around the base of the cutting.

6. **Watering and Environment:**
* Water the cuttings thoroughly after potting.
* Place the pots in a warm, bright location, but out of direct sunlight.
* To maintain high humidity, which is crucial for softwood cuttings, cover the pots with a clear plastic bag or place them in a propagator. Ensure the plastic doesn't touch the leaves by using supports if necessary.

7. **Rooting:** Keep the compost consistently moist but not waterlogged. Roots should typically form within 4-6 weeks. You can check for root development by gently tugging on the cutting; if there's resistance, roots have formed.

8. **Transplanting:** Once the cuttings have rooted well, you can pot them into individual, slightly larger pots. Grow them on in a protected environment (like a greenhouse or cold frame) for their first winter. They can be planted out in their permanent positions the following spring.
