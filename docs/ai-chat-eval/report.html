The Rhozly reply template — what "consistency" is judged against

Baked into the assistant's system prompt (agent-chat/rules.ts); canonical copy in reply-template.md.

Every Garden AI answer follows the same five-part shape, so a gardener always knows

where to look. Parts 2–5 appear only when they apply — a simple factual question

gets part 1 (and maybe 5) alone, in one or two sentences.

1. Bottom line — one plain sentence that directly answers the question or states

what has been prepared. Always first. Never a heading, never a preamble.

2. Detail bullets — only when the answer genuinely has parts (max ~6):

  • When: late summer, right after flowering
  • How: cut back a third, never into old wood
  • Watch for: frost on new growth

Short bold labels (When / How / How much / Why / Watch for) so the eye can jump

straight to the fact it needs.

3. Garden line — only when the assistant read the user's data this turn:

🔎 Checked: your Shed (2 tomatoes) · this week's tasks · weather

4. Action line — only when an action was staged for confirmation:

🔧 Ready to confirm: water Raised Bed A & B every 3 days

When a follow-up step depends on this one (e.g. linking an ailment that's being

added), the line also says what will be staged right after the user confirms.

5. Next step — at most one offer or question, on its own line:

→ Want me to set up a fortnightly feeding reminder too?

Why this shape

  • Bottom-line-first means nobody reads a paragraph to find a yes/no.
  • Labelled bullets make facts scannable and consistent across every answer.
  • 🔎 / 🔧 / → are fixed markers: what was *read*, what's *ready to apply*, what

*could happen next* — the same place, every reply.

  • One offer max keeps replies calm — no stacked questions.

The most cost-effective way to improve heavy clay soil is by incorporating organic matter. This can include compost, well-rotted manure, leaf mold, or even just grass clippings and shredded leaves. Here's a breakdown of why organic matter is so effective and how to use it:

* **Improves Soil Structure:** Clay soil has tiny particles that pack together tightly, leading to poor drainage and aeration. Organic matter acts like a sponge, creating larger aggregates and pore spaces, allowing water and air to penetrate more easily.
* **Enhances Nutrient Content:** As organic matter decomposes, it releases nutrients that plants need. It also improves the soil's ability to retain nutrients, preventing them from being leached away by heavy rain.
* **Supports Beneficial Microorganisms:** A healthy soil ecosystem is teeming with beneficial bacteria, fungi, and earthworms. Organic matter provides a food source for these organisms, which in turn further improve soil health.

1. **Compost:** This is arguably the best all-around soil amendment. You can make your own compost for free if you have a compost bin, or purchase it from garden centers. Spread a generous layer (2-4 inches) over the soil surface and then dig it in.
2. **Well-rotted Manure:** Animal manure, when fully composted, is an excellent source of nutrients and organic matter. Ensure it's well-rotted to avoid burning plant roots.
3. **Leaf Mold:** This is decomposed leaf litter and is particularly good for improving soil structure. It takes longer to produce than compost but is very effective.
4. **Green Manure:** Planting cover crops like clover, vetch, or rye and then digging them back into the soil before they set seed is a fantastic way to add organic matter and nutrients. This is a very low-cost option if you have the time and space.

Q Should I water deeply twice a week or lightly every day? Justify it.
For most established plants, watering deeply twice a week is generally better than watering lightly every day.

Here's why:

* **Encourages Deeper Root Growth:** Deep watering saturates the soil, encouraging plant roots to grow further down in search of moisture. This creates a stronger, more resilient root system that can better withstand dry spells.
* **Reduces Water Waste:** Light, frequent watering often only moistens the top layer of soil. Much of this water can evaporate quickly, especially in warm weather, meaning the plant doesn't get as much benefit.
* **Prevents Fungal Diseases:** Constantly wet soil surfaces can create an environment conducive to fungal diseases, particularly in humid conditions. Deep watering allows the soil surface to dry out between waterings, which is healthier for most plants.
* **More Efficient Nutrient Uptake:** A well-established root system that reaches deeper into the soil can access a wider range of nutrients.

**When light, daily watering might be considered:**

* **Newly Seeded Areas:** Seeds need consistent moisture near the surface to germinate.
* **Very Young Seedlings:** Delicate seedlings may have shallow root systems and can dry out quickly.
* **Extremely Hot and Dry Conditions:** In prolonged heatwaves, some plants may benefit from a surface misting in addition to deep watering, but this is supplementary.
* **Specific Plant Needs:** A few plants, like some ferns or bog plants, prefer consistently moist conditions.

**To determine the best approach for your garden, consider:**

* **Plant Type:** Different plants have different watering needs.
* **Soil Type:** Sandy soils drain quickly and may need more frequent watering than clay soils.
* **Weather:** Hot, dry, and windy conditions increase water needs.
* **Plant Maturity:** Established plants are generally more drought-tolerant than young ones.

A good rule of thumb is to check the soil moisture a few inches down. If it feels dry, it's time to water deeply.
