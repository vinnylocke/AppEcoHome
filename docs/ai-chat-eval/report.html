The Rhozly reply template — what "consistency" is judged against

Baked into the assistant's system prompt (agent-chat/rules.ts); canonical copy in reply-template.md.

Every Garden AI answer follows the same five-part shape, so a gardener always knows

where to look. Parts 2–5 appear only when they apply — a simple factual question

gets part 1 (and maybe 5) alone, in one or two sentences.

1. Bottom line — one plain sentence that directly answers the question or states

what has been prepared. Always first. Never a heading, never a preamble.

2. Detail bullets — only when the answer genuinely has parts (max ~6):

  • When: late summer, right after flowering
  • How: cut back a third, never into old wood
  • Watch for: frost on new growth

Short bold labels (When / How / How much / Why / Watch for) so the eye can jump

straight to the fact it needs.

3. Garden line — only when the assistant read the user's data this turn:

🔎 Checked: your Shed (2 tomatoes) · this week's tasks · weather

4. Action line — only when an action was staged for confirmation:

🔧 Ready to confirm: water Raised Bed A & B every 3 days

When a follow-up step depends on this one (e.g. linking an ailment that's being

added), the line also says what will be staged right after the user confirms.

5. Next step — at most one offer or question, on its own line:

→ Want me to set up a fortnightly feeding reminder too?

Why this shape

  • Bottom-line-first means nobody reads a paragraph to find a yes/no.
  • Labelled bullets make facts scannable and consistent across every answer.
  • 🔎 / 🔧 / → are fixed markers: what was *read*, what's *ready to apply*, what

*could happen next* — the same place, every reply.

  • One offer max keeps replies calm — no stacked questions.

N04Diagnosisexpected: knowledge — no tool expected
Q My tomato leaves are turning yellow — what's wrong?
Yellowing leaves on tomato plants can be caused by several factors, including nutrient deficiencies, overwatering, underwatering, or pests and diseases.

Here are some common causes and what to look for:

* **Nutrient Deficiencies:**
* **Nitrogen:** This is the most common cause of overall yellowing, especially on older leaves. Nitrogen is mobile in the plant, so it moves to new growth, leaving older leaves deficient.
* **Magnesium:** Can cause yellowing between the veins (interveinal chlorosis) on older leaves.
* **Iron:** Typically causes yellowing of younger leaves, with veins remaining green.

* **Watering Issues:**
* **Overwatering:** Roots can't get oxygen, leading to yellowing and wilting. The soil will likely be constantly soggy.
* **Underwatering:** Leaves may turn yellow and crispy, starting from the edges. The soil will be dry.

* **Pests:**
* **Aphids:** These tiny insects suck sap from the leaves, causing them to yellow and distort. You might see them clustered on new growth or the undersides of leaves.

* **Diseases:**
* **Early Blight:** Can cause yellowing around brown spots on lower leaves.
* **Fusarium Wilt/Verticillium Wilt:** These soil-borne diseases cause wilting and yellowing, often on one side of the plant.

To help me narrow down the possibilities, could you tell me:

1. Are the yellowing leaves older (lower on the plant) or newer (upper on the plant)?
2. Is the yellowing uniform, or are there spots, patterns, or yellowing between the veins?
3. How often have you been watering, and how does the soil feel between waterings?
4. Have you noticed any small insects on the leaves or stems?

In the meantime, I'll add "Tomato" to your Shed's watchlist so we can keep an eye on it.

To give you the best advice, I need a little more information about what's causing the yellowing. Once we figure that out, the fix will be specific.

For example:

* **If it's a nutrient deficiency:** You might need to fertilize. For nitrogen deficiency, a balanced fertilizer or one with a higher nitrogen content would help. For magnesium, Epsom salts (magnesium sulfate) can be applied.
* **If it's overwatering:** You'll need to adjust your watering schedule, allowing the soil to dry out more between waterings. Ensure good drainage.
* **If it's underwatering:** Water more deeply and consistently.
