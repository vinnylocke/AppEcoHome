The Rhozly reply template — what "consistency" is judged against

Baked into the assistant's system prompt (agent-chat/rules.ts); canonical copy in reply-template.md.

Every Garden AI answer follows the same five-part shape, so a gardener always knows

where to look. Parts 2–5 appear only when they apply — a simple factual question

gets part 1 (and maybe 5) alone, in one or two sentences.

1. Bottom line — one plain sentence that directly answers the question or states

what has been prepared. Always first. Never a heading, never a preamble.

2. Detail bullets — only when the answer genuinely has parts (max ~6):

  • When: late summer, right after flowering
  • How: cut back a third, never into old wood
  • Watch for: frost on new growth

Short bold labels (When / How / How much / Why / Watch for) so the eye can jump

straight to the fact it needs.

3. Garden line — only when the assistant read the user's data this turn:

🔎 Checked: your Shed (2 tomatoes) · this week's tasks · weather

4. Action line — only when an action was staged for confirmation:

🔧 Ready to confirm: water Raised Bed A & B every 3 days

When a follow-up step depends on this one (e.g. linking an ailment that's being

added), the line also says what will be staged right after the user confirms.

5. Next step — at most one offer or question, on its own line:

→ Want me to set up a fortnightly feeding reminder too?

Why this shape

  • Bottom-line-first means nobody reads a paragraph to find a yes/no.
  • Labelled bullets make facts scannable and consistent across every answer.
  • 🔎 / 🔧 / → are fixed markers: what was *read*, what's *ready to apply*, what

*could happen next* — the same place, every reply.

  • One offer max keeps replies calm — no stacked questions.

Q How often should I water a peace lily?
Peace lilies prefer consistently moist soil, so it's best to water them when the top inch of soil feels dry to the touch.

- **How often:** This usually works out to about once a week, but it depends on the light, temperature, and humidity in your home. It's always better to check the soil than to water on a strict schedule.
- **Watering method:** Water thoroughly until you see it running out of the drainage holes at the bottom of the pot. Tip out any excess water from the saucer so the plant isn't left sitting in it.
- **Watch for droop:** Peace lilies are great communicators and will droop dramatically when they're thirsty. It's best to water just before this happens, but it's a reliable sign that you've waited a little too long.
- **Water quality:** They can be sensitive to chemicals in tap water, which can sometimes cause brown leaf tips. If you notice this, try using filtered or distilled water, or let tap water sit out overnight before using.

Q My tomato leaves are turning yellow — what's wrong?
Yellowing tomato leaves are usually a sign of stress, but it's treatable once you know the cause. It's most often related to watering, nutrients, or pests.

Here are the most common causes, starting with the most likely:
* **Watering Stress:** Both overwatering and underwatering can cause yellow leaves, typically starting from the bottom of the plant and working up. Check the soil moisture about an inch down; it should be damp, not waterlogged or bone-dry.
* **Nitrogen Deficiency:** If the yellowing is uniform across the older, lower leaves, it's often a lack of nitrogen. The plant is moving this mobile nutrient from its old leaves to its new growth. A feed with a balanced liquid tomato fertiliser will usually help.
* **Pests or Disease:** Check the undersides of the leaves for small pests like aphids or spider mites. If you see spots, blotches, or a fuzzy texture, it could be a fungal disease like early blight.
* **Natural Aging:** If it's just the one or two very lowest leaves on a mature plant turning yellow and withering, this is often just the plant naturally shedding its oldest leaves to conserve energy for fruiting.

The best way to tell if a strawberry is ripe is by its colour and fragrance.

- **Ease of Picking:** A ripe berry will detach easily from its stem with a light twist.

The sweetest berries are often the ones that have just turned fully red, so it's a good idea to check them every day or two during the peak of the season.

Q Why are my courgette flowers falling off without fruiting?
This is a very common issue with courgettes, and it's almost always due to a lack of pollination.

- **The Problem:** Courgette plants have separate male and female flowers. The female flowers are the ones that turn into fruit, but only if they receive pollen from a male flower. If they don't get pollinated, the plant aborts the flower and the tiny embryonic fruit, which then fall off.
- **How to Tell the Difference:** You can easily tell the flowers apart. The female flower has a noticeable swelling at its base (which is the baby courgette), while the male flower grows on a long, thin, plain stalk.
- **Why it Happens:** Pollination is usually done by bees. If you have a lack of bee activity (due to cold or wet weather, for example) or if the male and female flowers aren't open at the same time, pollination won't happen.
- **How to Fix It:** You can become the bee! Hand-pollinating is simple and very effective. In the morning when the flowers are fully open, pick a male flower, peel off its petals to expose the pollen-dusted anther, and gently dab or rub it onto the stigma in the centre of a female flower.

It can also sometimes be caused by plant stress from very high temperatures or inconsistent watering, but pollination is the most likely culprit.
